Abstract

State-of-the-art machine learning weather prediction systems achieve strong upper-air forecast skill but degrade at longer lead times, with mesoscale structures progressively vanishing and ensemble forecasts becoming under-dispersive. While these limitations are often attributed to the training objective, we find that message-passing processors significantly contribute to this degradation: repeated neighbourhood aggregation at each autoregressive step acts as a smoothing operator that, over rollout steps, suppresses high-frequency information in latent space. This effect is particularly limiting in regional weather prediction, where fine-scale structures are already partially unresolved, further eroding mesoscale variability and hindering long-horizon forecasting. We propose GraphMet, a graph-based weather model motivated by Koopman theory that replaces stacked message-passing layers with a single linear Koopman operator step. GraphMet learns linear evolution in a spectral latent basis while preserving the encoder, decoder, and multi-scale icosahedral mesh structure. A two-term spectral objective jointly learns a stable Koopman basis and preserves latent geometry, enabling efficient long-range propagation without repeated nonlinear aggregation. Empirically, GraphMet reduces the 15-day activity deficit, improves regional RMSE by 11–14% at 5 km resolution, and enables efficient probabilistic forecasting at substantially lower inference cost than strong baselines. Motivation: Where MLWP Models Lose High-Frequency Structure

  • Modern MLWP models (GraphCast, AIFS, Pangu-Weather, GenCast) follow an encode-process-decode pipeline with a deep GNN processor at the Mesh2Mesh stage.
  • Each message-passing layer applies a row-stochastic aggregation matrix to node features, a local averaging operation. Repeating this \(L=16\) times per step is iterated averaging, formally proven to collapse Dirichlet energy and erase spatial structure.
  • Stacked across \(T=40\) autoregressive rollout steps, the contraction is catastrophic for mesoscale features (fronts, orographic precipitation, sea-breeze circulations).
  • This issue is commonly attributed to the MSE objective, but probabilistic / diffusion models (GenCast, GraphEFM, DiffLAM) exhibit the same failure — pointing to the architecture as the dominant cause.

We formalize this through Koopman operator theory: the latent covariance condition number \(\kappa(\mathbf{C}_\phi) = \lambda_{\max} / \lambda^{+}_{\min}\) directly controls the Koopman approximation error. Deep processors drive \(\lambda^{+}_{\min} \to 0\), discarding precisely the short-lived mesoscale modes that govern regional weather.

Proposed Method

GraphMet keeps the same Grid-to-Mesh encoder as in GraphCast/GenCast, the same multi-scale icosahedral mesh, and the same Mesh-to-Grid decoder. The single architectural change is the Mesh2Mesh processor, which is replaced by a single linear Koopman step \(\mathbf{z}^{t+1} = \mathbf{K}\,\mathbf{z}^{t}\), parameterised by a block-sparse matrix shared across the 14 edge-interaction types of the icosahedral mesh (12 directional + 2 land/ocean self-interactions). With \(m=32\), this gives only \(14\times 32^{2} = 14{,}336\) parameters, vs. \(\sim 10^{12}\) for a dense Koopman matrix.

Block-Sparse Koopman Processor

The processor performs one matrix-vector multiplication in the spectral latent basis, with \(k=14\) shared sub-matrices \(\mathbf{K}_\alpha \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times m}\):

\[ \mathbf{z}_{i}^{\,t+1} \;=\; \sum_{(j,i)\in\mathcal{E}_M} \mathbf{K}_{\alpha_{ij}}\,\mathbf{z}_{j}^{\,t} \]

Information propagates globally in one step thanks to the multi-mesh edge structure (refinements \(M_{0}\)–\(M_{6}\)), not through stacked local hops.

Spectral Training Objective

A three-term loss jointly learns a stable Koopman basis (\(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{spec}}\)), preserves latent geometry (isotropy penalty \(R\)), and anchors the decoder in physical space (\(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{phys}}\)):

\[ \mathcal{J}(\boldsymbol{\phi},\mathbf{K}) \;=\; -\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{spec}}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) \;+\; \xi_{1}\,\bigl[R(\hat{\mathbf{C}}_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(t)) + R(\hat{\mathbf{C}}_{\boldsymbol{\phi}}(t{+}1))\bigr] \;+\; \xi_{2}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{phys}}(\boldsymbol{\phi},\mathbf{K}) \]

Eckart–Young–Mirsky optimality (Theorem 1): at the minimum, the encoder recovers the leading-\(r\) Koopman singular functions, and \(\mathbf{K}^{\star}\) has the true singular values \(\sigma_{1},\dots,\sigma_{r}\) on its diagonal.

Analytic Probabilistic Forecasting via NCP

Because GraphMet explicitly recovers the Koopman left/right singular functions \(u_{i}, v_{i}\) and their decay rates \(\varsigma_{i}\), it integrates natively with the Neural Conditional Probability (NCP) framework. The transition density at lead time \(T\) admits the closed form:

\[ \mathbb{P}\bigl[\mathbf{z}^{\,t+T} \in B \,\big|\, \mathbf{z}^{\,t} = \mathbf{z}\bigr] \;\approx\; \int_{B} \Bigl(\,1 + \sum_{i=1}^{r} \varsigma_{i}^{\,T}\, u_{i}(\mathbf{z})\, v_{i}(\mathbf{z}')\,\Bigr)\, \mu(\mathrm{d}\mathbf{z}') \]

Conditional variances, probabilistic tracks and quantiles are computed by integrating test functions against this density — no diffusion sampling, no ensemble generation.
